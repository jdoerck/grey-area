# Esperanto and Hangul: two designed languages, two opposite outcomes

A research note on what each language tried to do, why one stalled and the other won, and what marain should take from both.

---

## Why these two

Both Esperanto and Hangul are designed languages. Both came from a single mind (or a small team behind one mind) with an explicit reform agenda. Both met institutional resistance. Both were declared, in the language of their inventors, to be tools of liberation: Zamenhof wanted to dissolve ethnic conflict; Sejong wanted to put literacy in reach of farmers. Beyond that the parallels stop. Esperanto is a whole language — phonology, morphology, syntax, lexicon — for which Zamenhof had to invent every word. Hangul is only a writing system; the spoken language was already there. That difference in scope ends up mattering far less than where each project sat in relation to power. Esperanto had no state. Hangul had a king. Five hundred years later, that fact is still doing most of the work.

This note covers the creation of each, the design choices each makes, the political environment each had to survive, and finally the lessons that fall out for marainkit — a deterministic display language with its own design pretensions and its own 100-year horizon.

## Esperanto: the surgeon from Białystok

Ludwik Lejzer Zamenhof grew up in Białystok in the 1860s and 1870s, when it was a city in the Russian Empire with four ethnic groups living in close quarters: Poles, Jews, Russians, and Germans. They did not get along. The young Zamenhof — a Jewish ophthalmologist by training — concluded, by his own account, that mutual incomprehension was upstream of the violence. If the four communities could speak to each other in a language none of them owned, none would feel demoted, and the prejudices would erode.

He spent roughly a decade building one. By 1885 he had a complete draft. He spent two years looking for a publisher and failed. In 1887, after his marriage to Klara Silbernik, his father-in-law put up the money from her dowry. A Warsaw printer named Chaim Kelter agreed to publish, and on 26 July 1887 the first edition appeared in Russian under the title *Mezhdunarodnyi yazyk* — "International Language." Zamenhof wrote under the pseudonym *Doktoro Esperanto* — "Doctor Hopeful." Within months, Kelter had also issued Polish, French and German editions. The pseudonym attached itself to the language, and *Esperanto* it became.

The first book — *Unua Libro* — had three parts: an introduction laying out the purpose, a grammar section presenting sixteen rules, and a dictionary of 917 root words. The 16 rules survived almost intact and were canonised in 1905, when the *Fundamento de Esperanto* was published and the first World Esperanto Congress at Boulogne-sur-Mer ratified the *Declaration of Boulogne*, which made the Fundamento the language's "single, perpetual obligatory authority." Every reform attempt since then has had to contend with that 1905 lock.

### Design choices

Esperanto's grammar is famously regular. Nouns end in -o. Adjectives end in -a. Adverbs end in -e. The infinitive of every verb ends in -i. Present tense is -as, past is -is, future is -os, conditional is -us, imperative is -u. There are no irregular verbs. There is no grammatical gender. Plurals add -j. Direct objects take an accusative -n. The morphology is agglutinative: roots stack with prefixes and suffixes (mal- for opposite, -ist- for an agent, -ej- for a place) and the result is fully predictable. *Lerni* is to learn, *lernanto* is a learner, *lernejo* is a school, *mallernanto* is — at least in principle — a non-learner. A learner who has internalised the affixes can guess at words they have never seen and usually be right.

This regularity is the reason Esperanto is genuinely fast to acquire. The Paderborn studies of the 1970s, run by Helmar Frank's Institute of Cybernetics, put the figure at around 150 hours for a French-speaking learner to reach a working level in Esperanto, against roughly 1,500 hours for English and ~2,000 for German under comparable conditions. Volapük, Esperanto's predecessor — invented by the German priest Johann Martin Schleyer in 1879 — required 300 to 500 hours by contemporary estimates. Esperanto won the constructed-language contest of the 1880s on simplicity and on Zamenhof's willingness to release control: Schleyer insisted on retaining proprietary rights over Volapük, and his community fractured around 1889.

But the regularity is on a Eurocentric chassis. Zamenhof's source languages were the ones he actually knew: Russian, Polish, Yiddish, German, French, Latin, with smaller draws from English, Greek and Hebrew. The phoneme inventory reflects this — /x/, /ʒ/, /ts/, the diphthong /eu̯/ — phonemes that are rare or absent in much of Asia, Africa and the Americas. Modern typological scoring puts Esperanto at roughly 75% feature-overlap with European languages and 54% with the world's average language. The vocabulary is overwhelmingly Romance and Germanic. *Patro* (father) is from Latin *pater*. *Birdo* (bird) is from English. *Knabo* (boy) is from German *Knabe*. A Mandarin speaker meeting Esperanto for the first time meets a slightly smoothed version of European grammar with an alphabet they have to learn. A Spanish speaker meets a familiar friend.

The script is Latin with five diacritic letters: ĉ, ĝ, ĥ, ĵ, ŝ, plus ŭ. The diacritics were a typesetting disaster from day one. Zamenhof had to invent a fallback ("h-system": cx, gx for ĉ, ĝ etc.) before computers even existed. Today a plurality of online Esperanto is written with the *x*-system because keyboards still cannot do diacritics fluently in many environments.

### Adoption arc

The first World Congress in Boulogne-sur-Mer in August 1905 drew 688 attendees from twenty countries and was, by every account, exhilarating. Through the 1910s and 1920s the movement grew quickly. National associations formed across Europe, Japan and Brazil. Radio Vienna broadcast in Esperanto. The League of Nations took up the question of adopting it as a working language in 1921. China's delegate — and ten others — supported it. France, fearing displacement of French as the language of diplomacy, vetoed. The motion stalled. It would never get that close to institutional power again.

The Soviet position is instructive. In the 1920s the Soviet government was favourable: Esperanto fit Bolshevik internationalism, the Soviet Esperantist Union (SEU) was founded in 1921, and Esperanto correspondence with foreign workers was officially encouraged. By the late 1930s Stalin had pivoted to "socialism in one country," and the same correspondence networks that had been an asset became evidence of foreign contact. Ernest Drezen, the SEU's leader, was shot on 27 October 1937. Vladimir Varankin was executed in 1938. Thousands of others were arrested, exiled, or worse. The Soviet Esperanto movement was effectively destroyed.

Hitler's *Mein Kampf* had already named Esperanto as a tool of Jewish world domination — Zamenhof was Jewish, and the family was visible. The Nazis banned Esperanto organisations after 1933. All three of Zamenhof's surviving children — Adam (shot at Palmiry, 1940), Zofia (deported to Treblinka, 1942), and Lidia (deported to Treblinka, 1942) — were murdered by the Nazi regime. The European movement, which had been the centre of gravity, was gutted in a single decade by both the regimes that had originally seemed to host it.

There was also an earlier, self-inflicted wound. In 1907 a body called the Delegation for the Adoption of an International Auxiliary Language convened in Paris to choose between competing constructed languages. Zamenhof was barred by procedural rule from representing Esperanto and sent Louis de Beaufront in his place. A reformed Esperanto was tabled, anonymously, under the pseudonym *Ido* ("offspring"). The committee endorsed Esperanto on the condition that it adopt the Ido reforms. De Beaufront turned out to have been the Ido author all along. The Esperanto community refused the reforms; about 20% of the movement's leadership and ~3-4% of ordinary speakers defected to Ido. The schism was small in numbers but it cost the movement years of credibility at exactly the moment it most needed unity.

### Why it stalled

Reduce all of this to a single sentence and the answer is: Esperanto needed a state and never got one. Languages are not adopted on merit. Languages are adopted because someone in power requires them — for school, for army service, for trade, for office work — and because the network of speakers crosses a threshold beyond which not learning the language costs you something. Esperanto crossed neither threshold. It got close in 1921 and got vetoed.

After 1945 the network problem hardened. English had the U.S. economy, Hollywood, postwar science publication, the internet, air-traffic control, and (later) every default in software. The case for Esperanto — that the world needed a neutral common tongue — was strongest when no natural language had won. By 1960 a natural language had won, imperfectly, and the gap closed. Esperanto today has perhaps two million speakers worldwide, of whom around 2,000 are native (*denaskuloj*) — children of mixed-language couples who chose Esperanto as the household language. Duolingo's English-medium course had 1.36 million registrations by 2018. The community is real. The language is alive. But it never became what Zamenhof wanted it to be, and the structural reasons it never did are mostly not about the language itself.

## Hangul: the king who ignored his court

In 1443, King Sejong of the Joseon dynasty completed a new alphabet for Korean. He promulgated it in 1446 in a document called *Hunminjeongeum* — "The Correct Sounds for the Instruction of the People." The accompanying explanatory text, *Hunminjeongeum Haerye*, lays out the principles. Sejong worked with scholars from the Hall of Worthies (*Jiphyeonjeon*), but the historical consensus — partly preserved in the *Haerye*, rediscovered only in 1940 — is that the design was substantially his own. This is unusual. Most writing systems evolved across centuries. Hangul has an author, a date, and a manifesto.

The motivation in *Hunminjeongeum*'s preface is unusually direct: Korean is not Chinese, the people cannot express themselves in Chinese script, and as their king, Sejong feels obliged to give them a script that fits their language. The political subtext is that the *yangban* — the Confucian scholar-aristocracy — held a monopoly on literacy through their command of Classical Chinese (Hanja), and that monopoly mapped onto a monopoly on bureaucratic and judicial power. Putting reading in reach of farmers and women was, in 15th-century terms, a redistribution.

### Design choices

Hangul is a *featural* script, which means the shapes of letters encode the phonological features of the sounds they represent. There are very few featural scripts in the world. Hangul is the most thoroughly worked-out one and the only one that became a national writing system.

The five basic consonants depict the configuration of the speech organs producing them. ㄱ /k/ is the root of the tongue blocking the back of the mouth — the letter is a stylised right-angle showing tongue against soft palate. ㄴ /n/ is the tongue tip touching the alveolar ridge — the letter is the L-shape of that contact. ㅁ /m/ is the closed lips — a square mouth. ㅅ /s/ is the teeth — a tooth shape. ㅇ /ŋ/ (originally also a glottal stop / null onset) is the open throat — a circle.

Aspirated and tense versions of these consonants are derived by adding strokes to the basic shape. ㄱ /k/ becomes ㅋ /kʰ/ with one extra stroke for aspiration; it becomes ㄲ /k͈/ when doubled for tenseness. The shape carries the feature. A reader who has learned the system can predict from the geometry what kind of sound a new letter represents — place of articulation from the family resemblance, manner of articulation from the strokes added.

Vowels are constructed from three primitives: a horizontal line (the earth, ㅡ), a vertical line (the human, ㅣ), and a dot (heaven, originally ㆍ, now a short stroke). The basic vowels stack these primitives in regular ways. ㅏ is human + dot to the right. ㅓ is human + dot to the left. ㅗ is earth + dot above. ㅜ is earth + dot below. Additional vowels are formed by combining these — ㅑ, ㅕ, ㅛ, ㅠ for the j-glide series; ㅐ, ㅔ, ㅘ, ㅝ etc. for diphthongs and combinations. The yin-yang philosophy underneath this — bright vowels (ㅏ, ㅗ) versus dark (ㅓ, ㅜ) — also tracks Korean vowel harmony, so the philosophy is doing real linguistic work, not decoration.

Letters are not written in a line. They are composed into syllable blocks: an initial consonant, a medial vowel, and an optional final consonant (the *batchim*) packed into a square. The block 한 ("han") contains ㅎ + ㅏ + ㄴ. The block 글 ("geul") contains ㄱ + ㅡ + ㄹ. Together they spell *Hangul* — "Korean script" — in two squares. Each block fits a minimum of two and a maximum of six letters, but the visual unit a reader's eye picks up is always the syllable. This is unusual: Hangul is alphabetic at the level of how it represents sound, but syllabic at the level of how it presents text. It gets the predictability of an alphabet and the visual density of a syllabary in one design.

The script Sejong promulgated had 28 letters. Four (ᄛ, ᅀ, ᅌ, ㆍ) fell out of use. Today's Hangul has 24 base letters (14 consonants, 10 vowels) plus combinations — 19 consonant phonemes and 21 vowels in standard South Korean orthography. The script has been revised but never replaced.

### Adoption arc

The court hated it. Choe Manri, a scholar of the Hall of Worthies, submitted a famous memorial against Hangul in 1444 arguing that abandoning Chinese characters would barbarise Korea, that the new script was vulgar, and that Sejong had created it without consulting the bureaucracy. Sejong overrode the objection. But Choe Manri was articulating what the *yangban* believed. For four hundred and fifty years they treated Hangul as a vernacular — fit for women, for Buddhist tracts, for popular fiction, but not for officials, courts, civil-service exams, scholarship, or law.

In 1504 the situation got worse. Anonymous broadsheets ("tuseo") appeared in Hanseong written in Hangul, criticising King Yeonsangun for tyranny. Yeonsangun ordered a total ban: no learning, no teaching, no writing in Hangul. Books in the script were burned. Knowing Hangul was a reportable offence. The ban was short-lived — Yeonsangun was deposed in 1506 — but the signal had been sent. Hangul stayed in the cultural basement for the next three centuries. It survived because women used it, because Buddhist monks used it, because vernacular fiction was written in it, and because everyday people kept it alive as a tool.

Two events pulled it out. The first was the Gabo Reform of 1894, in which a modernising government — partly under Japanese pressure during the First Sino-Japanese War, partly responding to the Donghak peasant uprising — declared that official documents would be written in Hangul (or in mixed script with Hanja). Schools began teaching in Hangul in 1895. The first Hangul newspaper, *Tongnip Sinmun* ("The Independent"), appeared in 1896. After 450 years in the basement, Hangul became, almost overnight, a tool of the modern state.

The second was the Japanese occupation, 1910-1945. The Japanese colonial government initially tolerated Hangul, then progressively suppressed it. By 1938, Korean was banned in schools. In 1942 the Japanese arrested members of the Korean Language Society — which had standardised Hangul orthography in 1933 and was working on a comprehensive Korean dictionary — accused them of independence agitation, and tortured several to death. The 1933 Unified Hangul Orthography (*Hangeul Matchumbeop Tongiran*) is the basis of today's South Korean spelling. The dictionary was published, posthumously in part, after liberation in 1945.

What occupation did, paradoxically, was finish the work that the Gabo Reform began. By the time the Japanese left, Hangul had become identified with Korean national identity in a way that 450 years of vernacular use had not produced. Mixed script (Hangul + Hanja) was the dominant style at liberation; over the next several decades, both Koreas progressively reduced Hanja, and South Korea now uses essentially pure Hangul outside specialist contexts. North Korea abolished Hanja almost immediately. Today Korea has near-universal literacy. Sejong got what he wanted — about 500 years late.

## What's actually different

Both Zamenhof and Sejong wanted to redistribute access. Both got institutional resistance from the people whose status was threatened. Both saw their work survive in cultural basements until a crisis pulled it out. The difference is not in the design briefs. It's in the relationship to power.

Zamenhof's design philosophy was radical neutrality. He wanted Esperanto to be no one's native language, owned by no government, easier than the existing options, and politically unaligned. Strategically this meant Esperanto could not cooperate with state power without compromising its core proposition. The closest it came was 1921 at the League of Nations and 1920s Soviet sponsorship, and both bets failed for political reasons that were ultimately about who states want speaking what.

Sejong's design philosophy was technocratic top-down reform. The script existed because a king willed it into existence, defended it against his own bureaucracy, and signed his name to it. Power was not a problem to be avoided; it was the engine. Hangul was unwelcome to the elite for centuries, but it had been declared official by the throne, the throne kept it official across regime changes, and when modernisation finally came in 1894 and 1910, Hangul was already in the toolbox — an alphabet that had survived 450 years of marginalisation precisely because Sejong had given it the *imprimatur* of the state and that imprimatur outlasted every faction that opposed him.

There is also a scope difference that matters. Esperanto bet everything on the idea that you could replace natural languages — or at least add a neutral lingua franca on top of them. That is an enormous ask. Hangul replaced nothing. Korean was already spoken; Sejong only changed how it was written. He bet on a substrate change, not a content change. Substrate changes are easier to win because they don't require people to give anything up; they only require that the new substrate be sufficiently better that switching costs pay back. Hangul's design — featural, articulatory, syllable-block — made it dramatically faster to learn than Hanja. The learning curve made the case. Esperanto's regularity made it faster to learn than French, but you would only learn it instead of French, and most people had no occasion to.

A final difference: Esperanto froze. The 1905 Boulogne Declaration locked the *Fundamento* as immutable. Reforms were rejected. Ido split off and was rejected. The language has accreted vocabulary but its grammar is exactly as Zamenhof shipped it. Hangul evolved. Letters dropped out. Orthography was standardised in 1933 by a society that did not exist when the script was created. North and South diverged; Latin transliteration schemes shifted multiple times in the 20th century. Hangul kept its core invariant — featural shapes, syllable blocks — and let everything else move. The one that stayed alive was the one that allowed change.

## Lessons for marain

Marainkit is not a spoken language. It's a deterministic display language — a rendering grammar where context inputs produce display outputs predictably. The analogy to Hangul is closer than the analogy to Esperanto: marain is a substrate, not a content replacement. But the failure modes from Esperanto are still warnings, and the survival modes from Hangul are still instructive.

**The substrate move beats the content move.** Esperanto tried to replace what people communicated *with*. Hangul changed only how an existing message was written down. Marain is closer to the second case: it sits on top of existing content (UI tokens, language fragments, status states) and only changes the rendering. That is the right scope. Resist any temptation to expand into "people should think in marain" territory. The minute the project asks people to give something up, it has Esperanto's adoption problem.

**Pick your substrate to be cheaper than the alternative, not just better.** Hangul beat Hanja because it took weeks to learn instead of years. Esperanto's edge over French was a factor of ten in learning time, but you didn't need French if you didn't engage internationally, and most people didn't. Marain's substrate proposition has to be: rendering is more reliable, drift is less costly, the tokens are clearer, the system is cheaper to maintain than the ad-hoc alternative. *Cheaper* is the operative word. "More elegant" does not move people.

**Iconicity earns its keep when it does double duty.** Hangul's shapes encode articulation, which means a learner can predict the sound of a new letter from its geometry. The shape is doing semantic work. Marain's glyph layer should aim for the same standard: the 9-bit binary glyph should not just be a code, it should encode something a reader can use — disambiguation, structural relationship, status escalation. Decorative iconicity is dead weight. Working iconicity is a multiplier.

**Lock the foundation. Let the surface move.** The *Fundamento de Esperanto* and the Boulogne Declaration killed Esperanto's ability to evolve in 1905. Hangul kept its featural-and-syllabic core invariant and let orthography, letter inventory and rendering medium drift. Marain's stable core should be the smallest possible — the principles in `direction.md` (token-driven, legibility first, context-explicit, substrate-independent), the glyph mapping, the status scale. Everything else — fonts, themes, encoding details, language vocabulary — should be free to move. Document the boundary explicitly. Future contributors will try to lock surface-layer choices into the foundation; that is how design projects ossify.

**Survive the wilderness years.** Hangul spent 450 years as a vernacular before becoming official. The reason it survived is that it stayed useful for women, monks and storytellers when nobody at court would touch it. If marain has any future, it needs an analogous niche — a small group of people for whom it is *actually the best tool*, even if no institution recognises it. The display-language framing helps here: a single team using marain for internal status surfaces, dashboards, or constructed-world worldbuilding (Culture-novel adjacent contexts) is a perfectly viable basement-level use case. Don't over-invest in convincing institutions. Invest in being the best tool for someone, anyone, doing real work.

**Network effects work both ways.** Esperanto died on the network problem: nobody learned it because nobody else had. Hangul partially escaped this because it was *additive* — you could write Hangul *or* Hanja or both, in any ratio, and gradually shift. Marain should be additive too: it should compose with existing systems (Latin, CSS tokens, JSON, whatever) rather than replace them. A "go all-in or stay out" design fails the same way Esperanto did. A "use as much as is useful, the rest stays" design is how Hangul actually got into the bloodstream.

**Authorial control is a vulnerability.** Schleyer killed Volapük by holding too tightly. Zamenhof avoided that mistake by releasing control early — the Fundamento was open, modifications were officially welcomed (even if the community then refused them all). Sejong's lever was different: he was a king, he could afford to be authoritarian in 1443, and he declared the work open to all classes by definition. Marain's directional documents and AI conventions already point this way (`direction/` as a shared knowledge base, no proprietary tooling, file formats readable without special software). Keep going. The 100-year horizon principle in `direction.md` is the right defence against the Schleyer trap.

**Politics will find you.** Zamenhof did not want Esperanto to be a Jewish project, an internationalist project, a Communist project, or a peace project. It became all four to its enemies, and the enemies killed it where they could. Sejong's project was a king's project from day one and that was its protection. Marain's politics — substrate independence, no platform dependency, no institutional dependency, 100-year horizon — are visible in `direction.md` and they are politics, even if they read as engineering principles. Be honest about that. The values are not platform-neutral; they imply opinions about platforms. Document the opinions.

## A short reading list

If you want to go deeper than this note:

- *Dangerous Language: Esperanto under Hitler and Stalin* — Ulrich Lins. The standard account of why both totalitarianisms targeted Esperanto.
- The *Fundamento de Esperanto* itself, in any decent edition, including the 16 rules. It is short. Read it once.
- *Hunminjeongeum Haerye* (Korean: 훈민정음 해례본) — the 1446 explanatory text, rediscovered in 1940. Several modern translations exist.
- Wikipedia's articles on Hangul, Esperanto, Volapük, Ido and the Korean Language Society are unusually well-sourced and will get you into the primary literature fast.
